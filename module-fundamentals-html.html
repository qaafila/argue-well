Introduction

What makes an argument truly compelling? In this module, we'll explore the fundamental components of strong arguments, drawing insights from Bo Seo's book "Good Arguments". By the end, you'll be able to identify and construct arguments that are clear, logical, and persuasive.

Components of a Good Argument

1. Claim

A claim is the main point or thesis of your argument. It's what you're trying to prove or persuade others to believe.

2. Evidence

Evidence is the factual information that supports your claim. This can include data, statistics, expert opinions, or real-world examples.

3. Reasoning

Reasoning is how you connect your evidence to your claim. It explains why the evidence supports your position.

4. Link

The link is how your argument connects to the larger context or why it matters. It answers the "so what?" question.

Examples from "Good Arguments"

Example 1: Climate Change

Claim: Immediate action on climate change is necessary.

Evidence: Global temperatures have risen by 1.1°C since the pre-industrial era.

Reasoning: This temperature increase is causing more frequent and severe weather events, rising sea levels, and ecosystem disruptions.

Link: If we don't act now, these changes will lead to irreversible damage to our planet and way of life.
